How We Built These New Email Tool Ratings

Before you trust any review site's ratings, you should know how they were made. Here is exactly what we did. Between January and June 2026, we created real accounts on every platform, imported the same list of 500 opted-in subscribers, and sent identical test campaigns through each tool. We did not use sandbox environments or simulated sends — everything went to real inboxes so we could measure real deliverability rates.

We scored each platform on six criteria: ease of onboarding (how fast you could send your first email), template quality (did they look professional without heavy editing?), deliverability (what percentage of our emails actually landed in the primary inbox rather than spam?), automation depth (how many steps could you build without hitting a paywall?), customer support (did a human answer within a reasonable time?), and overall value for money (what do you actually get for each dollar you spend?).

Each criterion was scored out of 5. We averaged the scores to produce a composite rating. Then we weighted value for money slightly higher because that is the factor that matters most to the small business owners we serve. You can see the full breakdown in the comparison table below.

We also cross-referenced our scores against data from Litmus's 2026 Email Deliverability Report and feedback from over 200 small business owners who responded to our annual reader survey. This is not a one-person opinion. It is a structured process, and these new email tool ratings reflect it.

Mailchimp Review: The Famous Name That No Longer Leads

Mailchimp is probably the most recognised name in email marketing, and there are good reasons it built that reputation. The platform has a polished interface, a broad template library, and a free plan that is genuinely useful for very small lists (under 500 contacts). For complete beginners who want to try email marketing at zero cost, Mailchimp is still worth a look.

The problem is what happens as you grow. The moment your list passes 500 contacts, Mailchimp's pricing jumps to $13/month on its Essentials plan — but at that tier, you lose some features you might assume are standard, like the ability to remove Mailchimp branding. To access A/B testing beyond subject lines, you need the Standard plan at $20/month. For advanced segmentation and comparative reporting, you are looking at $350/month on the Premium tier. That pricing ladder catches a lot of small business owners off guard.

Deliverability in our test came in at 91.2% primary inbox placement — solid, but about 6 points behind Constant Contact. Support quality was also noticeably weaker. Our test queries went to a chatbot first, and escalation to a human agent took 47 minutes during business hours. That is a frustrating experience when you are under time pressure.

Mailchimp scores 4.2/5 in our new email tool ratings. It is a good tool, but not the best tool for most small businesses at this price point. See our detailed Constant Contact vs Mailchimp comparison for a side-by-side breakdown of every major feature.

MailerLite Review: Budget-Friendly but with Real Gaps

MailerLite earns a 4.1/5 in our new email tool ratings. It is the most affordable paid option in this roundup, with plans starting at $9/month for up to 500 subscribers. The free plan allows up to 1,000 subscribers and 12,000 monthly email sends, which is genuinely generous and makes MailerLite worth considering if your budget is extremely tight.

The platform's email editor is clean and reasonably easy to use, though it takes a bit longer to get comfortable with than Constant Contact's. The automation builder on paid plans lets you create multi-step welcome sequences and basic conditional logic, which is more than most free tools offer.

Where MailerLite falls short is support and deliverability. Support is email-only on the free plan and live chat plus email on paid plans — there is no phone option. During our test, response times averaged 3.5 hours. That is acceptable if you are not in a rush, but it is a gap compared to Constant Contact's near-instant responses. Deliverability came in at 89.4% primary inbox placement, the second-lowest in our test.

MailerLite is a reasonable choice for hobby bloggers or solo operators sending a simple monthly newsletter. For a small business that relies on email to drive actual revenue, the support gaps and deliverability deficit make it a riskier option than Constant Contact. Compare the two directly in our Constant Contact vs MailerLite comparison.

ActiveCampaign Review: Great Power, Wrong Audience

ActiveCampaign scores 4.0/5 overall in our new email tool ratings. That lower overall score does not mean it is a bad product — in fact, for automation depth alone, ActiveCampaign gets a perfect 5.0/5. No other platform in this roundup comes close to the complexity of workflows you can build: conditional branching, lead scoring, CRM integration, predictive sending, and dozens of other features that large businesses and marketing agencies rely on.

The issue is that all that power comes with a steep learning curve and a higher price tag. ActiveCampaign's Starter plan begins at $15/month, but to access most of the automation features that make the platform unique, you need the Plus plan at $49/month or the Professional plan at $79/month. For a small business owner who wants to send a weekly newsletter and the occasional promotional email, that is far more than you need to spend.

Ease of use scored 3.5/5 — the lowest in our roundup. The interface has improved significantly over the past two years, but it still assumes a level of marketing knowledge that most small business owners do not have when they are first getting started. If you are running a solopreneur business or a team of fewer than five people, you will spend more time learning ActiveCampaign than using it.

Deliverability came in at 92.1% — strong, but not at the level of Constant Contact. If you are curious how these two compare feature by feature, read our Constant Contact vs ActiveCampaign comparison.

ActiveCampaign earns its place in our new email tool ratings as the best option for established businesses that are ready to invest in serious marketing automation. For everyone else, it is overkill.

Brevo Review: Interesting Pricing Model, Inconsistent Execution

Brevo — formerly known as Sendinblue — takes a different approach to pricing than every other tool in our roundup. Instead of charging based on the number of contacts you store, Brevo charges based on the number of emails you send per month. The free plan allows up to 300 emails per day. Paid plans start at $9/month for 5,000 monthly sends.

This pricing model is genuinely useful for businesses that have large contact lists but only email them occasionally. If you have 5,000 contacts but only send a monthly newsletter, Brevo's approach could save you money compared to contact-based pricing models. That is the platform's biggest selling point, and it is a real one.

Unfortunately, the rest of the experience does not match the cleverness of the pricing model. Brevo's template library is the smallest and least polished in our test — many templates look dated compared to what Constant Contact and Mailchimp offer. The email editor works, but it feels clunky, and we ran into a rendering bug when trying to add a button block during our review period.

Deliverability scored 88.7% primary inbox placement — the lowest in our roundup. That number matters. If 11 out of every 100 emails you send never reach the inbox, you are leaving real money on the table every time you hit send. Support response times averaged 4.2 hours, and the knowledge base is less thorough than what Constant Contact and even MailerLite provide.

Brevo scores 3.8/5 in our new email tool ratings. It earns a spot in this roundup as the right pick for a very specific type of business — one with a large but infrequently emailed list — but for most small businesses, there are better options at a similar or lower price point.
