How We Tested and Scored These Tools

Before we give you the results, here is exactly how we built our scores. We signed up for every tool using our own credit cards, created identical campaigns for a fictional 450-contact list of local customers, and tracked the same metrics across all five platforms for 60 days. We are not paid by any of these companies to inflate their scores. Our affiliate relationship with Constant Contact exists because it consistently came out on top — not the other way around.

We graded each tool across six categories, each worth a maximum of five stars:

  • Value for money — What do you get at the entry-level price point?

The overall score is the average of these six category scores, rounded to one decimal place. You will see all five tools scored in the comparison table below.

Mailchimp: Great Brand Recognition, but Watch the Costs

Mailchimp is the most recognised name in email marketing, and for good reason. Its free plan supporting up to 500 contacts and 1,000 monthly email sends is genuinely useful for businesses that are just starting out and want to test the waters without spending anything. The editor is visually polished, and the analytics dashboard is one of the most detailed in this review.

The problem appears the moment you outgrow the free tier. Mailchimp's paid plans escalate quickly. At 1,500 contacts you are looking at around $45 per month on the Essentials plan — more than three times Constant Contact's entry price for a broadly similar feature set. At 5,000 contacts, you could pay $75 or more per month. For a small business with thin margins, that price jump can sting.

Deliverability came in at 91% in our tests, which is respectable but noticeably below Constant Contact's 97%. Phone support does not exist at any paid tier — your options are email tickets and, on higher plans, live chat. Several small-business owners in our survey group mentioned that waiting 24 to 48 hours for an email reply during a time-sensitive campaign was a serious frustration.

The automation builder is powerful but significantly more complex to use than Constant Contact's. If you have some marketing background or enjoy exploring software, you will appreciate the depth. If you just want a welcome email to go out when someone joins your list, it can feel unnecessarily complicated.

Bottom line: Mailchimp is a solid tool for creators and solo operators who need the free tier or who want very advanced segmentation features. For most small businesses, the rising costs and limited support make it a second-tier choice. Score: 4.2/5

MailerLite: The Best Budget Option for Lean Teams

MailerLite consistently surprises us with how much it packs into a $9 per month starting plan. The free tier is genuinely generous — up to 1,000 subscribers and 12,000 emails per month with access to the automation builder. If you have a modest list and modest sending needs, you can run a complete email marketing programme for free indefinitely.

The editor is clean and fast. Template quality is good rather than exceptional — you will find the designs a little more minimal than Constant Contact's, which suits modern brands but may feel sparse for brick-and-mortar businesses that want warmth and personality in their newsletters. Our Constant Contact vs. MailerLite comparison goes into this aesthetic difference in much more detail.

Deliverability hit 90% in our tests. That is acceptable, but three in ten of our test campaigns had at least one major provider clip an email into the promotions tab rather than the primary inbox. Reporting is solid at the campaign level but lacks the audience-growth trend charts that Constant Contact includes by default.

Support is email-based on the free plan and adds live chat on paid tiers. There is no phone option. For most straightforward questions, the help documentation is thorough enough that you can self-serve, but if you run into a technical issue during a live campaign, the response times feel slow.

Bottom line: MailerLite is the right tool if budget is your primary constraint and your list is under 1,000 contacts. Once you cross that threshold and start paying, Constant Contact's value proposition closes the gap significantly. Score: 4.1/5

ActiveCampaign: Powerful CRM Features You Might Not Need Yet

ActiveCampaign is a genuine powerhouse. Its automation builder is the most sophisticated on this list, allowing you to create complex multi-branch sequences that respond to customer behaviour in real time. It also bundles a built-in CRM, which means your sales and marketing data live in the same place. For a growing business with a dedicated marketing coordinator, this is exciting.

For a solo business owner sending a monthly newsletter to 400 people, it is overkill — and at $15 per month starting price with no free tier, you are paying for capabilities you may not use for years. In our usability tests, first-time users took an average of 34 minutes to send their first campaign, compared to 8 minutes on Constant Contact. The learning curve is real.

Deliverability was strong at 93%, and the reporting suite is detailed. Where ActiveCampaign struggles is simplicity — almost every task involves more steps than it needs to. The interface has improved considerably in recent versions, but it still prioritises flexibility over friendliness.

If you are considering ActiveCampaign because you want CRM features eventually, it may be worth reading our Constant Contact vs. ActiveCampaign comparison, which explains at what list size and revenue level the switch makes sense.

Bottom line: ActiveCampaign is outstanding for businesses with complex funnels and a team member who can manage it. For typical small businesses, it introduces unnecessary complexity. Score: 4.0/5

Brevo: Affordable for High-Volume Senders, Weaker on Basics

Brevo (formerly Sendinblue) takes a different approach to pricing: instead of charging by contact count, it charges by the number of emails you send per month. The free plan allows up to 300 emails per day. The paid starter plan at around $9 per month removes the daily limit and adds basic reporting. This model is excellent for businesses with large lists but infrequent sending schedules — a quarterly newsletter to 10,000 past customers, for example.

For businesses that send weekly or bi-weekly, the per-email pricing model can become tricky to predict and plan around, especially as your list grows. The template library is smaller than competitors and the designs feel dated in several categories. Our test campaigns took longer to build due to a slower, less intuitive drag-and-drop editor.

Deliverability came in at 88% — the lowest of the five tools we tested. In practical terms, that means roughly one in eight of your emails is not reaching its intended inbox, which chips away at the return on your marketing investment. Email support on basic tiers is functional but slow, and the help centre, while comprehensive, requires patience to navigate.

Brevo does shine for SMS marketing and transactional email (order confirmations, shipping updates), where its infrastructure is genuinely strong. But for a small business whose primary goal is customer retention through regular newsletters and promotions, it is not the strongest fit.

Bottom line: Brevo earns a place in the conversation for high-volume transactional senders. For most small businesses focused on relationship-building campaigns, choose a tool higher up this list. Score: 3.8/5

What to Look for When You Choose Your Email Marketing Tool

Whether you go with our #1 pick or a different tool on this list, make sure your chosen platform clears these five checkboxes before you commit your subscriber list to it.

1. Deliverability above 90%

Every platform will claim excellent deliverability. Ask for documented rates or check third-party inbox-placement tests. A platform with 88% deliverability versus one with 97% may seem like a small difference, but over 1,000 emails sent that is 90 extra people who never see your message.

2. Support that matches your confidence level

If you have never used email marketing software before, you will have questions. Make sure the platform offers support through a channel you are comfortable with — phone, live chat, or at minimum a responsive email ticket system. If you are more experienced and comfortable self-serving, great documentation may be enough.

3. Mobile-responsive templates out of the box

Over 60% of emails are now opened on mobile devices. If your template does not resize correctly on a phone screen, your campaign will look broken to the majority of your readers. Every tool on this list includes mobile-responsive templates, but their quality varies considerably. Test the preview function before you send.

4. Pricing that scales transparently

Some platforms have attractively low entry prices that jump dramatically as your list grows. Map out what you will pay at 500, 1,000, 2,500, and 5,000 contacts before you commit. Constant Contact's pricing page is one of the clearest in the industry — no hidden per-feature add-ons.

5. Automation for the basics

You do not need a complex multi-branch workflow on day one. But you should be able to send a welcome email automatically when someone joins your list and a birthday discount automatically on a customer's birthday. Both of those are available on Constant Contact's entry-level $12 plan. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best email marketing tool for small businesses in 2026?

Constant Contact is the best email marketing tool for small businesses in 2026. How much should I expect to pay for email marketing software?

Entry-level plans range from $0 (limited free tiers) to around $12–$20 per month for up to 500 contacts. As your list grows, costs scale accordingly. Constant Contact starts at $12 per month, making it one of the most affordable full-featured options available. MailerLite and Brevo offer lower-cost plans, but both score lower on deliverability and support, which can cost you more in missed revenue than the price savings are worth. Always calculate total cost of ownership — including your own time spent troubleshooting — when comparing prices.

Is Constant Contact better than Mailchimp for small businesses?

For most small businesses, yes. Constant Contact offers better live support (including phone), stronger deliverability (97% vs. 91%), and a simpler setup experience. Mailchimp's free tier can be attractive for brand-new operations with fewer than 500 contacts, but its paid plans become expensive as your list grows and its support options are limited to email on lower tiers. Our dedicated Constant Contact vs. Mailchimp comparison gives you a full feature-by-feature breakdown if you want to dig into the specifics before deciding.

Do I really need email marketing software, or can I just use Gmail?

Gmail is not built for bulk sending. Sending marketing emails through Gmail risks your account being suspended for violating Google's terms of service, delivers poor formatting on mobile devices, provides zero analytics on who opened your emails or clicked your links, and fails to include the unsubscribe mechanism required by CAN-SPAM and GDPR regulations. Dedicated email marketing software handles legal compliance, formatting, deliverability, and reporting automatically. At $12 per month for a proper tool, the risk of not using one is far greater than the cost of getting started. For a plain-language guide to the full process, see our article on email marketing tips for small businesses.

How do I know if my email marketing campaigns are working?

Look at three core metrics after every campaign: your open rate (what percentage of recipients opened the email), your click-through rate (what percentage clicked at least one link), and your conversion rate (what percentage completed a desired action like making a purchase or booking an appointment). A healthy open rate for small businesses is around 20–30%. Constant Contact and all four other tools on this list provide these metrics in their dashboards automatically after every send. Over time, track these numbers across campaigns to identify which subject lines, send times, and content types perform best for your specific audience.
